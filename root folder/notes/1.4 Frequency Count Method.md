1.4 Frequency Count MethodVideo Link: Duration: 12 Minutes, 22 SecondsCore ConceptThe Frequency Count Method is a technique used to analyze the Time Complexity of an algorithm. Instead of measuring time in seconds (which varies by machine), we count the number of times each statement in the algorithm is executed relative to the input size $n$.Key Analytical StepsAssign Units: Every simple statement (assignment, arithmetic, return) is considered 1 unit of time.Analyze Loops: * A loop that runs from $1$ to $n$ executes its condition $n+1$ times (to account for the final false check) and its body $n$ times.Nested Loops: If a loop of $n$ is inside another loop of $n$, the inner statements execute $n \times n = n^2$ times.Summation: Add the frequency of all statements to get a polynomial expression.Order of Growth: Identify the highest degree term (e.g., $n, n^2, \log n$) to determine the Big-O complexity.Examples Covered in the VideoSum of Array Elements: A single loop traverses the array once. The total frequency is $2n + 3$, leading to a complexity of $O(n)$.Matrix Addition: Uses two nested loops (rows and columns). The frequency count results in a polynomial where the highest term is $n^2$. Complexity: $O(n^2)$.Matrix Multiplication: Uses three nested loops (rows, columns, and the dot product summation). This results in a frequency count dominated by $n^3$. Complexity: $O(n^3)$.Note: This method focuses on the "Time" aspect of "Time and Space Complexity." It teaches you to look at the structure of the code (loops and nesting) to predict how the algorithm will scale.
